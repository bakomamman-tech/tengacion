# Tengacion Certification 30/60/90 Day Execution Roadmap

Follow-on roadmap:
[Tengacion Institutional 30/60/90 Day Execution Roadmap](./tengacion-institutional-30-60-90-roadmap.md)

## Purpose

This roadmap is the fifteenth execution cycle after the foundation, operating-system, scale, expansion, platform, ecosystem, network, intelligence, automation, orchestration, resilience, assurance, compliance, and audit roadmaps. The earlier cycles made Tengacion usable, operable, launchable, expandable, platformized, ecosystem-ready, network-aware, intelligence-led, automation-ready, orchestration-capable, resilience-tested, assurance-backed, compliance-mapped, and audit-ready. This cycle is about turning audit readiness into credible external trust:

1. Certification strategy and external review scope
2. Trust center, evidence rooms, and claim governance
3. Security, privacy, data, access, and vendor certification readiness
4. Finance, payout, creator earnings, partner, API, content, and market review readiness
5. Akuso and AI governance external-readiness evidence

The goal is not to claim certification before Tengacion has earned it. The goal is to choose the right external review path, close the highest-risk gaps, maintain evidence continuously, and make every public, partner, investor, creator, finance, security, privacy, or AI claim traceable to tested proof. Formal certification, legal compliance, and regulated-market claims should be reviewed by qualified assessors and counsel.

## Starting Baseline

This plan assumes the previous roadmap has produced these anchors:

- an audit universe with auditable domains, control families, owners, evidence sources, risk scores, review cadence, and readiness state
- evidence rooms for finance, payments, payouts, privacy, security, vendors, content rights, moderation, partner access, APIs, external reporting, recommendations, automations, and Akuso
- tested controls with sampling rules, pass/fail criteria, reviewer notes, retest rules, and closure evidence requirements
- audit findings with severity, root cause, impacted obligations, owners, due dates, remediation plans, compensating controls, and verification status
- control owner certifications for critical money, data, access, rights, partner, API, market, and Akuso workflows
- external review packets for partner due diligence, security review, privacy review, finance review, API review, AI governance review, and market readiness review
- an Akuso audit trail connecting model routes, prompts, policy versions, source context, evals, feedback, refusals, memory behavior, incidents, and cost controls

The remaining problem is external trust maturity. Tengacion can test controls internally, but it still needs to decide which external reviews matter, what evidence can be shared, which claims are allowed, which findings block external commitments, how evidence stays fresh between reviews, and how certification work becomes part of operating rhythm instead of a one-time scramble.

## North-star Outcomes For The Next 90 Days

By the end of this plan, Tengacion should have:

- a certification and external review strategy that ranks candidate paths by business value, user trust, partner demand, market pressure, risk, cost, and readiness
- a trust center operating model with approved public claims, restricted evidence rooms, partner packets, security and privacy summaries, finance and creator trust summaries, and AI governance summaries
- a claims register that maps every external claim to owner, source evidence, review date, sharing level, expiration, and withdrawal path
- certification gap plans for security, privacy, data protection, access review, vendor governance, incident response, finance controls, creator earnings, partner/API access, content rights, moderation, and Akuso
- continuous evidence maintenance for critical controls so certification readiness does not decay after the review window
- external-review playbooks for assessors, partners, investors, enterprise customers, creator groups, payment providers, sponsor reviews, and market readiness reviews
- control evidence automation priorities for logs, access reviews, vendor reviews, finance close, rights workflows, partner/API access, data contracts, and Akuso evals
- an AI governance external-readiness packet for Akuso that clearly states scope, limits, source grounding, privacy boundaries, eval process, human review, incident response, and known limitations
- a decision on whether Tengacion is ready to pursue a formal certification, a targeted external assessment, a partner due diligence program, or another internal hardening cycle

## Product Principles

- External trust must be earned: do not publish trust claims that exceed tested evidence.
- Certification should follow business need: choose external reviews that unlock real partners, markets, creators, users, or risk reduction.
- Claims expire: every external claim needs an owner, evidence date, review date, and withdrawal rule.
- Evidence should stay alive: certification readiness should rely on continuous controls, not last-minute screenshots.
- Share only what is safe: public, partner, investor, auditor, and restricted evidence need different boundaries.
- Findings should gate commitments: open high-risk issues should block external claims, access expansion, and market promises.
- Finance and creator trust matter as much as security: creator earnings, payouts, refunds, disputes, fees, and balance confidence need external-ready evidence.
- AI claims require extra humility: Akuso should be described by tested capabilities, guardrails, limitations, sources, evals, and human review paths.

- create a certification and external review strategy
- choose the first external review candidates
- define the trust center and claims governance model
- map certification gaps by domain
- prepare external-ready evidence standards and reviewer workflow

  - investor due diligence

  - creator earnings review

## Akuso Certification Readiness Roadmap

Akuso should only be represented externally through tested, narrow, evidence-backed claims.

### Phase 1: External Claim Boundaries

- define what Akuso is:
  - in-app assistant
  - product guidance layer
  - creator writing helper
  - support triage helper
  - incident-aware guidance layer where source context permits
- define what Akuso is not:
  - payment executor
  - payout approver
  - legal decision-maker
  - rights decision-maker
  - moderation final decision-maker
  - partner access approver
  - account security operator
  - source of truth for finance, compliance, or incident status
- map each external claim to source evidence, eval evidence, owner, review date, and withdrawal rule

### Phase 2: AI Governance Packet

- prepare external-safe summaries for:
  - capability scope
  - model and vendor approach
  - source grounding
  - eval process
  - privacy and memory boundaries
  - high-risk refusal boundaries
  - human review
  - incident response
  - feedback loop
  - cost and routing controls
  - known limitations
- keep sensitive prompts, private incidents, security detail, user data, model secrets, and policy-sensitive notes restricted

### Phase 3: Mock AI Review

- test reviewer questions about:
  - data use
  - model routes
  - prompt changes
  - eval failures
  - hallucination handling
  - unsafe answer handling
  - privacy and memory handling
  - incident mode
  - human review
  - user appeal or complaint path
- convert weak answers into findings, evidence gaps, policy updates, or product fixes

### Phase 4: Ongoing AI Evidence Maintenance

- refresh eval reports, source coverage, feedback summaries, incident summaries, unsupported claim tracking, refusal quality, privacy/memory samples, and cost routing summaries
- expire claims when evals or source evidence go stale
- block new AI external claims until source, eval, privacy, and refusal evidence is current
- include AI governance in selected external review or diligence packets where relevant

## Decision Rules

- If a claim cannot point to current evidence, do not publish or share it.
- If the external scope is unclear, narrow it before sharing evidence.
- If evidence is restricted, do not summarize it externally without approved language.
- If high-risk audit findings remain open, pause formal certification claims and narrow external review scope.
- If finance evidence is weak, do not make creator earnings, payout, balance, or settlement trust claims.
- If privacy, deletion, retention, access, vendor, or security evidence is weak, pause data sharing, API expansion, partner access, and AI grounding claims.
- If partner/API revocation evidence is weak, do not expand external access.
- If content rights or moderation evidence is weak, do not expand content-category, market, campaign, or recommendation claims.
- If Akuso source, eval, privacy, refusal, memory, incident, or cost evidence is stale, do not expand AI claims.
- If the team is overloaded, protect this order:
  - security, privacy, access, data, and vendor evidence
  - finance, payment, payout, creator balance, refund, dispute, and settlement evidence
  - partner, API, export, and external report evidence
  - content rights, moderation, support, and market evidence
  - Akuso and AI governance evidence
  - broader trust center polish and lower-risk external summaries

## What Not To Do Yet

- do not claim formal certification until a qualified external process has been completed
- do not use internal audit readiness language as a public certification claim
- do not share restricted evidence through public trust center content
- do not publish AI claims that exceed Akuso eval, source, privacy, refusal, and incident evidence
- do not pursue broad external review when high-risk internal findings are still unresolved
- do not choose certification paths because they sound impressive; choose them because they unlock real trust, risk reduction, or business need
- do not let trust center content become stale marketing copy
- do not over-share security, privacy, incident, prompt, user, partner, finance, or policy-sensitive details
